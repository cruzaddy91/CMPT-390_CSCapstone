# Proposal Requirements

Here is an explanation of the requirements of the final proposal:

If you are proposing a research project you should propose a thesis (a hypothesis) to address. Then modify the following sections to describe the work you plan to investigate your thesis.

**The abstract** is a 2-6 sentence single paragraph. It is an overview of the entire proposal. It should succinctly state the problem being addressed, the purpose, the methodologies planned, and desired goals. It is not simply the first paragraph of the paper; in fact, it's often a good idea to save writing the abstract for last! Please look at the resources on writing abstracts linked below for more information on what an abstract is.

**The introduction** should lay out the key ideas behind your project. This section should include what your project will be, your goals and objectives, emphasizing what you plan to accomplish, not how you will do it.

**The background section** should establish the context for the project, including a review of previous work in this area and an explanation of how your project fits into it. Try to organize previous work into sensible categories, rather than providing a list of relevant projects. For example, if you are proposing a scheduling program, describe some existing scheduling programs, including aspects you want to copy or improve. Also discuss the relevant technologies/languages/tools you will use, and demonstrate that you have considered appropriate options.

**The proposed work section** is the longest part of your proposal. The details of the proposal are in this section. You should provide the following information and organize it along these lines:

- **Specific tasks**

  A brief, but specific list of tasks needed to accomplish the project. As opposed to restating the goals (what you plan to do), you should emphasize how you plan to complete your project. The task list should demonstrate that you have carefully investigated your problem and have a clear understanding of how to attack it. This list of tasks should be concise statements of what needs to be done. Elaborate on the details of the task in the plan of work sub-section.

- **Rationale**

  Explain why this work should be done and why it should be done the way it is being proposed.

- **Plan of work**

  A procedural description of the tasks you intend to pursue: what will be done, how? Do not provide excessive details. You want to provide enough information to show you have thought through the problem and have developed an approach with some reasonable chance of success.

- **Preliminary work (optional)**

  Include any work you have already completed or other work that demonstrates the feasibility of your project.

- **Deployment strategy (new this year - not present in example proposals)**

  Describe how your completed project will be made available for "real-world" use. Identify where and how it could be deployed (i.e., locally, in the cloud, or on specific platforms), who the intended users are, and any considerations that would influence your deployment choice. Or use this section to explain why deployment isn't applicable to your project.

- **Timeline**

  Create a project timeline that builds on the iterative development approach you learned in Software Engineering, selecting a sprint length that fits your project's scope and workflow. Your timeline should include a list of major tasks and milestones, with estimated start and completion dates, including but not limited to:

  - December 1, 2025 - Proof of Concept
  - March 13, 2026 - Beta Release
  - April 20, 2026 - Code Freeze

  The timeline will outline how the project will progress through the semester to successful completion. Outline where you expect to spend your time. Try to specify small increments of time for greater success (even down to blocks of anticipated hours). The timeline will likely underestimate some aspects and not foresee complications that arise when you do the project, but it will be a very useful guide to measure your own progress. It will help you refine your work and let you know if you need to revise plans as you go.

  **The Proof of Concept** is a minimal but functional demonstration that shows the core idea of your project is technically feasible. It should implement the essential feature or process that proves your approach will work, even if many components are still incomplete or only simulated.

  **The Beta Release** is a working program that can be demoed to users, even if some minor features still need to be implemented or debugged.

  **The Final Code Freeze** is the date at which your capstone project should be complete, so that you can work on the project demo, report, and presentation.

**The evaluation section** should describe how you will evaluate the success of your project, including both technical performance and potential ethical or social impacts on users or society. Discuss what criteria or metrics you will use to determine whether your project meets its goals, and how you will gather feedback or test your system.

**The conclusion** should be a brief paragraph that summarizes the significance of the problem, your proposed approach, and what impact you expect your project to have. This is a conclusion to the proposal, so focus on the project's goals and anticipated contributions.

You can include additional sections (i.e., prior work, references) if they are applicable to your proposed project. For example, CIS/BCIS majors may wish to include an executive summary for their proposals.

You may also add sub-sections to a required section. For example, the background section could have two sections:

**Background and rationale for the project:**

Why do you think this issue needs to be investigated? What will we learn? What will we gain? Why is it important? What are the implications? This section should mention existing software systems that are similar to what you are proposing or previous research related to the topic area.

**Methodological background**

Describe how you think you will develop your software project or investigate your research topic. In this section, you should pay particular attention to formulating a plan of action that you can pursue. You may also need to consider whether your plan is feasible. The content of this section will range widely. For software projects, this section should provide background on the technologies you will be using, explaining why they are the best choice. For research projects, this project should explain what data you will be collecting to analyze to test your hypothesis. (This section differs from the proposed work because this section explains why you using this technology, whereas the rationale in the proposed work explains why you are breaking up the project into the stages/phases that you've decided on.)
